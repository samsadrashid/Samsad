04

Key Design Decisions

These were the hardest calls, where UX had to meet financial risk, system logic, and product growth.

Transaction Layer

Why Amar Hishab, not just dashboard improvements

Improving the dashboard would not change user behavior deeply. Amar Hishab created daily utility through transfers, real-time balance, and transaction history.

Business Date Logic

Calendar date vs ERP business date

Users should interact with familiar calendar dates. The system detects ERP mismatches and warns users before confirmation.

Error States

Designing failure before success

In a financial product, failed transfers, insufficient balance, timeout, and duplicate transfer are core UX paths, not edge cases.

Non-Client Access

Why explore mode should not require login

For acquisition, product exploration must happen before registration. Non-clients can understand value before commitment.

01 — Overview

Making insurance feel less like insurance

Nirbhik is Bangladesh's first insurance marketplace — a B2B2C platform where users can discover, compare, and purchase policies across Life, Health, Accident, Travel, Vehicle, Electronics, and Fire insurance from multiple providers in a single unified experience.

The brief was straightforward but the stakes weren't: make insurance feel approachable. For a market where less than 1% of people hold any policy, the design had to do more than look good — it had to dismantle the psychological barrier to the first purchase.

Homepage and onboarding — designed to work on any screen size

02 — Business Context

Business Context

Insurance adoption in Bangladesh is limited by low trust, low awareness, complex policy language, and manual sales-led distribution. At the same time, young digital users, business employees, gig workers, and partner-brand customers are increasingly exposed to risk through mobility, travel, electronics, health needs, and work-related activities.

Nirbhik's opportunity sits at the intersection of low insurance adoption, growing digital behavior, business demand for employee protection, insurer need for digital distribution, and the absence of a strong embedded insurance infrastructure layer in Bangladesh.

The origin story

Born from the question: "How do we make insurance more accessible to young people in Bangladesh?" Nirbhik is a new-age insurtech platform founded in 2025, built for the brave, the bold, and the underserved.

What "Nirbhik" means

Nirbhik means fearless. The platform exists to make insurance effortless for individuals, businesses, and insurers — simple, seamless, and accessible.

Strategic framing

Most insurance platforms sell policies as a separate product. Nirbhik's strategy was different: make insurance appear at the exact moment of relevance.

07 — Process

Confidence as the design north star

The breakthrough insight came from a question users kept asking during research sessions: "Am I covered enough?" Not "What does this policy cover?" — but a more fundamental anxiety about adequacy.

This led to the Nirbhik Confidence framework — a coverage health score built around four life dimensions: Life insurance, Health (HO), Hospitalisation Coverage (IPD), and Accident protection. Sliders let users set their coverage targets; the system shows them their protection gap and what it would cost to close it.

The IA was restructured around this insight. Rather than leading with product categories (the insurer's mental model), we led with the user's situation — and surfaced categories as the answer to a coverage need, not as the starting point.

09 — Design Decisions

Key design decisions, challenges & resolutions

Decisions

01
Design insurance around life moments, not policy categories.

Users understand risk through situations like buying a bike, traveling, or protecting a family more easily than through insurance terminology.

02
Make embedded insurance almost invisible.

Partner journeys should not feel interrupted; protection should feel like a product benefit.

03
Use progressive disclosure for forms.

Insurance requires data, but showing all fields at once increases drop-off and anxiety.

04
Treat claims as the core trust journey.

Users judge insurance most when they need help, not when they purchase.

05
Separate portals by responsibility.

Consumers need clarity, business admins need control, insurers need policy and claim visibility, and ops teams need operational command.

Challenges & resolutions

Challenge

Insurance language creates fear.

Resolution

Use plain explanations, coverage summaries, examples, FAQs, and simple exclusions.

Challenge

Different categories require different data.

Resolution

Design modular enrollment with dynamic fields and category-specific steps.

Challenge

Embedded journeys need brand consistency.

Resolution

Keep partner branding dominant and position Nirbhik as the invisible protection layer.

Challenge

Businesses need flexible rules.

Resolution

Support configurable product access, subsidies, bulk upload, and reporting.

Challenge

Claims require coordination across users, ops, and insurers.

Resolution

Create guided claim workflows with checklists, notes, escalation, and status tracking.

Nirbhik taught me that designing insurance for an underserved market is not only about making forms easier. It is about designing for trust. Nirbhik is not just an insurance marketplace. It is a trust infrastructure layer for digital insurance in Bangladesh.

02

The World Cup got bigger. Fan tools didn't.

The 2022 edition had 32 teams and 64 matches. The 2026 edition has 48 teams and 104 matches — spread across three host nations and 16 venues. Yet the tools fans reach for weren't redesigned for this complexity.

The deeper problem isn't just information access. It's engagement architecture. Most football apps treat fans as passive consumers of data. There's no prediction mechanic, no social proof, no way to build a personal narrative through the tournament. Fans check a score, close the app, and lose the thread.

The core tension: more matches = more data = more cognitive overhead — yet the available tools were designed for 32-team simplicity. The gap between tournament size and tool quality was the design opportunity.

Information fragmentation
Fans check fixtures on one site, standings on another, and predictions on Twitter — three apps for one tournament.
No bracket investment
Without a personal stake in results, fans disengage after their national team exits. No prediction mechanic = no reason to stay.
Team discovery gap
With 16 new nations, there's no lightweight way to explore squads, kits, and rankings before watching their matches.
Bracket disorientation
The new Round of 32 confused casual fans. Existing apps showed results, not context.

03

Not every fan wants the same thing

Three archetypes mapped from community patterns and direct observation — each with a distinct relationship to tournament data.

⚽
The Follower
Casual · Score-checker

Watches 2–3 big matches. Checks scores after the fact. Uses WhatsApp groups to discuss results. Wants fast, frictionless access.

"I just want to quickly see scores and know who's through to the next round. Everything else is noise."
📊
The Analyst
Engaged · Prediction-builder

Watches most matches. Tracks group tables obsessively. Has opinions on every team's chances. Wants prediction history and bracket state.

"I want to build my bracket, lock in my predictions, and be proved right — or at least have the receipts when I am."
🌍
The Explorer
Curious · Team-discoverer

Neutral or multi-team fan. Excited by the 48-team expansion. Wants to know about unfamiliar nations before watching their matches.

"I don't know half these teams. I want to quickly read about them and pick one to support through the tournament."

A common design trap: designing for the Analyst and under-serving the casual fan. I made the deliberate choice to lead with the Follower experience (fast fixtures view on launch) while building depth for the Analyst and Explorer as secondary tabs — each complete on its own, never interrupting the others.

05

Six calls that shaped the product

Each decision is explained through the tradeoff it resolved, what was designed, and what it produced.

01 Mobile-first sticky bottom nav, not top tabs ↓
Tradeoff
Top tab bars are the web convention. Bottom bars feel unnatural on desktop and can conflict with browser chrome.
What was designed
Floating pill-shaped bottom nav with six tabs: FIXTURES · VOTE · TEAMS · BRACKET · BOARD · MORE. Dark mode toggle and user avatar in top-right header.
Outcome
Football is watched on mobile. Thumb reach to bottom tabs is dramatically easier. Sidebar on desktop preserves hierarchy without wasted whitespace.
02 Chained bracket system locked to group-stage winners ↓
Tradeoff
Open bracket pickers give maximum freedom but turn a prediction mechanic into arbitrary fantasy picking — every choice equally meaningless.
What was designed
Tree View (R32→R16→QF→SF→Final) and List View toggle. Share and Reset in header. Predicted World Champion as summary card at bottom.
Outcome
Constrained brackets are more engaging — every choice has a consequence because it determines the next round pool. Realism makes the mechanic feel higher-stakes.
03 Score prediction inputs default to 0–0 with minus locked at zero ↓
Tradeoff
Empty inputs force active choices — cleaner UX principle. But in football, 0–0 is a valid scoreline. Empty = broken state: what does "no prediction" mean?
What was designed
Inputs initialise at 0. Minus button disabled at 0 (prevents negative scores). Plus increments freely. Default state represents a real prediction, not an empty form.
Outcome
Eliminates broken state. Every user who opens the vote screen is implicitly predicting a 0–0 draw unless they change it — reducing drop-off from form friction.
04 Teams tab uses a Groups / Teams toggle, not two nav entries ↓
Tradeoff
Separate nav tabs for Groups and Teams would give each more screen space. But six tabs were already at the limit — a seventh would crowd the bottom bar.
What was designed
Toggle within Teams tab switches between Groups view (12 expandable group tables) and Teams view (48 nations in a grid sorted by FIFA ranking with search).
Outcome
Groups and team rosters are mentally linked — a fan exploring Group E wants to immediately jump to a team in that group. The toggle preserves that context jump.
05 Groups expand inline, not in a modal ↓
Tradeoff
Modals for expanded group tables feel focused and clean. But modals interrupt context, require a deliberate close, and break the spatial relationship between groups.
What was designed
Groups in 2×2 grid. Tapping a group expands inline standings table directly below — no modal, no navigation. Qualification spots (top 2) marked with green dot on team row.
Outcome
Fans compare standings across groups — "is Group A tighter than Group C?" Inline expansion lets multiple groups open simultaneously. Modal would have made that impossible.
06 Match Details as a modal overlay, not a new page ↓
Tradeoff
A dedicated match detail page is the conventional approach — easier routing, more space. But navigating away from Fixtures breaks the user's browsing flow.
What was designed
"View Details" opens a modal with: final score, FULL TIME badge, timestamped goal scorers, and "Your Prediction" block comparing the user's call with CORRECT / WRONG badge.
Outcome
Fans drill into a result and return instantly — no back navigation, no scroll position loss. Prediction comparison inside the modal closes the Vote mechanic loop with instant feedback.

06

Built to scale from prototype to production

The stack was chosen so Phase 1 (static/in-memory) and Phase 2 (real-time/database) share the same component architecture — zero migration cost when going live.

Phase 1 — Portfolio build

Runs on static data: all 48 squad rosters, group fixtures, and match results bundled directly into the app. State managed in-memory — no database dependency, no auth. Fast to prototype, safe to deploy without API costs.

Phase 2 — Live production

API-Football polls server-side at 60–90s intervals → Node.js crawler on Railway → Supabase. Supabase Auth enables a real leaderboard with prediction accuracy tracked across the tournament. The frontend never calls the sports API directly, protecting rate limits and keeping client code clean.

The choices that shaped the system

These weren't the only decisions but they were the moments where I had to push back, rethink, or take a non-obvious direction.

Booking Status
Why two states, not one
Single "Booked" state VS Tentative + Confirmed

Sales needed flexibility to hold rooms during negotiation without blocking availability permanently. The two-state model reduced cancellations by giving both parties a shared contract point. Color differentiation (amber vs green) made status scannable at a glance without reading text.

Booking Detail View
Slide-in panel vs. new page
Navigate to detail page VS Slide-in panel, stay in calendar

The calendar took 3–5 seconds to reload at scale. Navigating away and back disrupted the scanning workflow. A slide-in panel kept context intact — operations staff could check details without losing their 30-day view.

Architecture
Modular by domain, not by role
Role-specific portals VS Shared modules + permissions

Stakeholders pushed for separate portals per team. I resisted: siloed portals would recreate the coordination problem digitally. Shared modules with role-based access meant everyone saw the same data with different actions available.

The Unplanned Pivot
The folio redesign mid-stream
Original: single flat folio VS Split folio system

Three months in, finance raised a blocker: corporate bookings needed separate billing for events vs rooms. We had to redesign the folio module mid-stream. The split-folio system eliminated the #1 billing dispute between teams.

06

The choices that shaped the platform

Decision 01
Readiness model vs. simple player database

A simple player database would be faster to build, but it would not support real selection decisions. I designed a readiness model combining fitness status, injury condition, bowling workload, role, squad assignment, and availability — making the platform decision-grade rather than record-grade.

Decision 02
Bowling workload as a dedicated module

Bowling load could have been buried inside player details. Instead I surfaced it as a standalone module with player-wise load records, date filters, workload summaries, and warning states — making cricket's highest physical-risk area impossible to overlook.

Decision 03
Shared platform vs. separate portals

Separate portals would recreate fragmentation digitally. I designed a shared platform with role-based access so every team works from one source of truth — while actions remain controlled, and no role sees data it does not need.

Decision 04
Warnings before confirmation

The system needed to catch bad decisions before they were made — not after. I introduced pre-confirmation alerts and acknowledgement patterns that surface injury, restriction, unavailability, or overload before a squad is finalised.
