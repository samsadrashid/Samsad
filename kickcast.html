01 · Overview

One tab to rule the tournament

What Kickcast is, what I owned, and how the product fits into how fans actually watch football.

The FIFA World Cup 2026 is the first to feature 48 teams — a 50% expansion over 2022 — spread across three host nations (USA, Canada, Mexico) and 16 venues. For fans, this created a new problem: the tournament is too large to track casually. With 104 matches across a month-long window, keeping up with group stages, fixture times, team form, and bracket progression is cognitively expensive.

Kickcast is a web-based companion app I designed and built end-to-end — concept, architecture, visual language, interaction design, and production deployment. The product solves one primary problem: giving a football fan a single place to follow the World Cup with context, investment, and joy.

Unlike official tournament apps that prioritise broadcast scheduling, or score aggregators that show raw data without engagement mechanics, Kickcast was designed around the emotional arc of a football fan — anticipation, group drama, knockout tension, and the ritual of prediction.

My framing for this project: "A World Cup app isn't a utility — it's a companion to the biggest party on earth." The design's job isn't to show data. It's to make a fan feel like they're already at the stadium every time they open their phone.

I led every layer of this project: defining the feature set, building the information architecture, designing all UI components, writing production code in Next.js and Supabase, and deploying to Vercel. This is a solo portfolio build — not a client project — which gave me the freedom to design and build without constraint, and the responsibility of making every decision myself.

02 · Problem Space

The World Cup got bigger.
Fan tools didn't.

The 2026 expansion created friction across every touchpoint fans use to follow the tournament.

The 2022 World Cup in Qatar had 32 teams and 64 matches. The 2026 edition has 48 teams and 104 matches. That's not a minor update — it's a restructuring of how the tournament flows. There are now four new groups (12 total), a new Round of 32, and matches spread across three time zones simultaneously.

Yet the tools most fans reach for — Twitter/X threads, Wikipedia group tables, the FIFA official app — were not redesigned for this complexity. The official FIFA app is primarily a broadcast-scheduling product. Third-party score apps are real-time only, with no context or narrative. Sports media sites load slowly and are cluttered with ads.

The deeper problem isn't just information access. It's engagement architecture. Most football apps treat fans as passive consumers of data. There's no mechanism for prediction, no social proof around opinions, no way to build a personal narrative through the tournament. The result: fans check a score, close the app, and lose the thread.

The core tension: more matches = more data = more cognitive overhead — yet the available tools were designed for 32-team simplicity, not 48-team complexity. The gap between tournament size and tool quality was the design opportunity.

Signals from the field

Information fragmentation
Fans check fixtures on one site, standings on another, and predictions on Twitter — three apps for one tournament.
No bracket investment
Without a personal stake in results, fans disengage after their national team exits. No prediction mechanic = no reason to stay.
Team discovery gap
With 16 new nations debuting or returning, there's no lightweight way to explore squads, kits, and rankings before matches.
Bracket disorientation
The new Round of 32 confused casual fans. When does the knockouts stage start? Which teams advance from groups? Existing apps showed results, not context.
03 · Research & Archetypes

Not every fan wants the same thing

I mapped three archetypes from casual observation, online community patterns, and my own football-watching experience — each with a distinct relationship to tournament data.

⚽
The Follower
Casual · Score-checker

Watches 2–3 big matches. Follows their national team and one club favourite. Checks scores after matches, not during. Uses WhatsApp groups to discuss results.

"I just want to quickly see scores and know who's through to the next round. Everything else is noise."
📊
The Analyst
Engaged · Prediction-builder

Watches most matches. Tracks group tables obsessively. Has opinions on every team's chances. Discusses form, head-to-heads, and squad depth with friends.

"I want to build my bracket, lock in my predictions, and be proved right — or at least have the receipts when I am."
🌍
The Explorer
Curious · Team-discoverer

Neutral or multi-team fan. Excited by the 48-team expansion. Wants to know about unfamiliar nations — squads, shirts, history — before watching their matches.

"I don't know half these teams. I want to quickly read about them and pick one to support through the tournament."

What the archetypes mapped to

The three archetypes share the same need — follow the tournament — but at very different depths. The Follower needs fast, frictionless access to scores and standings. The Analyst needs persistent state (bracket saves, predictions) and structured data. The Explorer needs rich content — squads, kits, rankings — surfaced in a lightweight, discoverable format.

A common design trap for sports apps is designing for the analyst and under-serving the casual fan. I made the deliberate choice to lead with the Follower experience (fast fixtures view on launch) while building depth for the Analyst and Explorer as secondary tabs — each complete on their own, never interrupting each other.

This informed the six-tab navigation structure: Fixtures · Vote · Teams · Bracket · Board · More. Each tab has a single job. A Follower can use only Fixtures and be fully served. An Analyst will live in Vote, Bracket, and Board. The MORE tab surfaces player stats and scorer tables for deep-dive moments. No tab bleeds into another's responsibility.

05 · Design Decisions

Six calls that shaped the product

Each decision is explained through the tradeoff it resolved, why it mattered, what was designed, and what it produced.

01 Mobile-first navigation as sticky bottom bar, not top tabs ↓
Tradeoff
Top tab bars are the web convention for multi-section apps. Bottom bars are a mobile native pattern that feels unnatural on desktop, and can conflict with browser chrome.
What was designed
Floating pill-shaped bottom navigation with six tabs: FIXTURES · VOTE · TEAMS · BRACKET · BOARD · MORE. Each tab has an icon + label. The active tab is visually distinct. Dark mode toggle and user avatar sit in the top-right header.
Why it mattered / outcome
Football is watched on mobile. Thumb reach to bottom tabs is dramatically easier than stretching to top. The sidebar on desktop preserves hierarchy without wasted whitespace. Both feel native to their context.
📱
Bottom nav pill — FIXTURES · VOTE · TEAMS · BRACKET · BOARD · MORE with dark/light mode toggle
02 Chained bracket system locked to group-stage winners ↓
Tradeoff
Open bracket pickers (pick any 48 teams for any slot) are simpler to build and give users maximum freedom. But they turn a prediction mechanic into a fantasy picker — all choices become equally arbitrary.
What was designed
Bracket tab offers Tree View (visual bracket diagram with rounds across columns: R32 → R16 → QF → SF → Final) and List View toggle. Share and Reset actions live in the header. Predicted World Champion displays at the bottom as a summary card. 31/31 matches decided shown as progress.
Why it mattered / outcome
Constrained brackets are more engaging than open ones — every choice has a meaningful consequence because it determines the pool for the next round. This is how a real bracket works. Realism makes the mechanic feel higher-stakes.
🏆
Bracket — Tree View showing R32 → R16 → QF → SF → Final with predicted champion Brazil at bottom
03 Score prediction inputs default to 0–0 with minus locked at zero ↓
Tradeoff
Score inputs defaulting to empty force the user to make active choices — cleaner UX principle. But in football, 0–0 is a valid and common scoreline. An empty input creates a broken state: what does "no prediction" mean?
What was designed
Score inputs initialise at 0 for both teams. The minus button is disabled at 0 (prevents negative scores). Plus button increments freely. This means the default state represents a real prediction (0–0 draw), not an empty form.
Why it mattered / outcome
Eliminates the broken state of "no prediction submitted." Every user who opens the vote screen is implicitly predicting a 0–0 draw unless they change it — reducing drop-off from form friction. The disabled minus at zero also prevents input errors silently.
04 Teams tab uses a Groups / Teams toggle instead of a separate nav entry for each ↓
Tradeoff
Splitting Groups and Teams into two separate nav tabs would give each more dedicated screen real estate. But with six tabs already in the nav, adding a seventh would crowd the bottom bar and force users to context-switch between two related concepts.
What was designed
A toggle within the Teams tab switches between Groups view (12 expandable group tables showing 4 flag icons per group) and Teams view (all 48 nations in a 3-column grid sorted by FIFA ranking with a search field). Both views live under one nav tap.
Why it mattered / outcome
Groups and team rosters are mentally linked — a fan exploring Group E wants to immediately jump to a team in that group. Keeping them in one tab with a toggle preserves that context jump without a full navigation gesture. The nav stays clean at six tabs.
👕
Teams tab — Groups toggle (12 group tables) and Teams toggle (48 nations grid with search)
05 Groups expand inline, not in a modal or new page ↓
Tradeoff
Modals for expanded group tables are the common pattern — they feel focused and clean. But modals interrupt context, require a deliberate close action, and break the spatial relationship between groups that fans rely on to understand tournament structure.
What was designed
Groups sit in a 2×2 grid. Tapping a group expands an inline standings table directly below the group card — no modal, no navigation. Tapping again collapses it. Qualification spots (top 2) are indicated with a green dot on the team row.
Why it mattered / outcome
Fans often compare standings across groups — "is Group A tighter than Group C?" Inline expansion preserves the ability to open multiple groups simultaneously and compare them within one scroll. The modal pattern would have made comparison impossible and broken the mental map of the tournament bracket.
📊
Groups tab — 2×2 grid with inline expansion
06 Match Details modal with goal scorers — not a new page ↓
Tradeoff
A dedicated match detail page is the conventional approach — easier routing, more screen space. But navigating away from the Fixtures feed breaks the user's browsing flow when they want to check one result and continue scanning others.
What was designed
Tapping "View Details" opens a modal with: final scoreline, FULL TIME badge, winner label, timestamped goal scorers for each team (e.g. Kai Havertz 45'+6, 88'), and a "Your Prediction" block comparing the user's call with a CORRECT / WRONG result badge.
Why it mattered / outcome
Fans can drill into a result and return instantly to scanning — no back navigation, no scroll position loss. The prediction comparison inside the modal closes the loop between prediction and result, reinforcing the Vote mechanic with instant feedback.

06 · Technical Architecture

Built to scale from prototype to production

The stack was chosen so Phase 1 (static/in-memory) and Phase 2 (real-time/database) share the same component architecture — zero migration cost when going live.

Phase 1 — Portfolio build

The tournament-ready version runs on static data: all 48 squad rosters, group fixtures, and match results are bundled directly into the app. State (bracket picks, score predictions) is managed in-memory — no database dependency, no auth. This made the build fast to prototype and safe to deploy without API costs.

Key technical decisions: in-memory state instead of localStorage (sandbox compatibility), Barlow Condensed for score displays (condensed numerals read faster in tight card layouts), and a responsive breakpoint at 768px that switches navigation paradigm rather than just resizing.

Phase 2 — Live production

The planned production architecture replaces static data with API-Football (server-side polling at 60–90s intervals to stay within rate limits) feeding into a Supabase database. Supabase auth enables a real user leaderboard with prediction accuracy tracked across the tournament. The Node.js crawler service sits on Railway, acting as a data bridge between the sports API and Supabase.

This separation of concerns — crawler → Supabase → Next.js app — means the frontend never calls the sports API directly, protecting rate limits and keeping client-side code clean.

What this project proved

Kickcast is the most complete end-to-end project in my portfolio — not because it was commissioned, but because it went through every stage a production product goes through: research framing, feature architecture, visual system, component build, responsive engineering, automated data pipeline, and live deployment.

The most concrete signal of success: 20+ real users showed up on the leaderboard without any marketing push. They predicted match scores, tracked accuracy, and competed across the tournament. That's the engagement hypothesis validated in production — fans want a stake in the outcome, not just a score feed.

The project also proves I can own both design and code at a lead level: the chained bracket system, the Match Details modal, the real-time data pipeline via API-Football → Railway → Supabase — every architectural decision was a UX decision made visible through the build.

What I would do differently

The biggest gap left is a notification layer. The data pipeline is already fully automated — API-Football polls on a schedule, Railway crawls, Supabase stores — but there's no push or email trigger telling users a match they predicted is about to kick off. That's the feature most likely to drive return visits, and the Supabase auth + preferences table is already in place to support it.

I'd also add a social layer to predictions — letting users see what percentage of Kickcast fans predicted the same scoreline as them, not just who won the poll. That kind of social proof creates conversation, and conversation is what keeps a sports app alive between matches.
